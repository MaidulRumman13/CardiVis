Heart Disease

Heart disease, widely known as Cardiovascular disease (CVD), is the leading cause of death globally, killed approximately 17.9 million people each year, accounting for over 32% of all fatalities. Coronary heart disease, cerebrovascular illness, rheumatic heart disease, and other heart and blood vessel problems are all classified as CVDs. Heart attacks and strokes account for four out of every five CVD fatalities, with one-third of these deaths occurring before the age of 70. Heart disease can sometimes be "silent," with no indications or symptoms until a person has a heart attack, heart failure, or arrhythmia. Symptoms that may occur as a result of these occurrences include.

## Chest pain or discomfort, pain in the upper back or neck, indigestion, heartburn, nausea or vomiting, excessive exhaustion, upper body discomfort, disorientation, and shortness of breath are all symptoms of a heart attack.

## Fluttering sensations in the chest are known as arrhythmia (palpitations).

## Shortness of breath, weariness, or swelling of the feet, ankles, legs, abdomen, or neck veins are all symptoms of heart failure.


Chest Pain

There are four different forms of chest discomfort that people might feel.
(1) Typical Chest Pain: Typical (classic) chest pain is defined as first substernal chest pain or discomfort that is secound triggered by physical activity or emotional stress and third eased by rest or nitroglycerine (or both).

(2) Angina: Angina is a form of chest discomfort caused by a reduction in cardiac blood flow. Angina is a symptom of coronary artery disease.
Squeezing, pressure, heaviness, tightness, or discomfort in the chest are common symptoms of angina, commonly known as angina pectoris. Angina symptoms include chest pain and discomfort that might feel like pressure, squeezing, burning, or fullness.

(3) Non-anginal pain: Non-anginal pain is a word used to describe chest discomfort that is not caused by heart disease or a heart attack. Non-cardiac chest discomfort is usually caused by an oesophageal issue, such as gastroesophageal reflux illness. Muscle or bone disorders, lung illnesses or diseases, stomach issues, stress, anxiety, and depression are some of the other reasons.

(4) Asymptomatic: Asymptomatic heart attacks, also known as silent myocardial infarction (SMI), account for 45-50 percent of cardiac morbidity and even premature mortality in India each year. Males are twice as likely as females to acquire SMI among middle-aged persons. Because the symptoms of SMI are so minor compared to those of a heart attack, it is known as the "silent killer." Unlike the signs of a typical heart attack, which include severe chest pain, stabbing pain in the arms, neck, and jaw, abrupt shortness of breath, perspiration, and dizziness, the symptoms of SMI are fleeting and commonly misdiagnosed as ordinary discomfort.


Electrocardiogram(ECG)

An electrocardiogram (ECG) is a basic test that checks the rhythm and electrical activity of your heart. The electrical impulses produced by your heart each time it beats are detected by sensors placed to your skin. ECGs (electrocardiograms) and EKGs (electrocardiograms) are two terms for the same thing. However, ECG has certain limitations. It monitors heart rate and rhythm, but it doesn't always reveal artery obstructions.


ST segment /heart rate slope (ST/HR slope)

The ST/heart rate slope (ST/HR slope), which measures the ST segment shift in relation to exercise-induced increases in heart rate, has been proposed as a more reliable ECG criteria for identifying severe coronary artery disease (CAD).


Cholesterol

Cholesterol is a waxy molecule that may be present in your bloodstream. Cholesterol is required by your body to produce healthy cells, but excessive cholesterol levels might raise your risk of heart disease.
You might have fatty deposits in your blood vessels if you have high cholesterol. These deposits eventually accumulate to the point that blood flow through your arteries is restricted. Sometimes, such deposits might rupture unexpectedly and create a clot that causes a heart attack or stroke.
